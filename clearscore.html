ClearScore — Recommendations that read as advice, not advertising

UI/UX Designer · B2C acquisition · Fintech · CRO

01Context

At GAIN Conversion I was the UI/UX designer optimising ClearScore’s B2C acquisition funnel for credit brokerage, focused on the homepage hero and highest-traffic pages. I worked in a delivery pod with a PM, UX consultant, UI developer and QA.

02Challenge

The brief was simple and unforgiving: lift sign-ups. Traffic was overwhelmingly mobile, the brand leaned on distinctive natural imagery, and every idea had to earn its place through a live 6–8 week experiment — no opinions, only results.

03Process

I studied the traffic first, confirmed mobile dominance, and optimised every targeted hero mobile-first. We tested moving-image versus still backgrounds, and leaned into ClearScore’s recognisable mountains, lakes and forests to evoke calm and reinforce brand. We ran experiments on button copy, H1/H2 wording, and added lender logo badges beneath the hero for trust.

When the UX consultant pushed wordy headlines, I pushed back — my read was that length would cost conversion. So we tested it.

Short vs long — tested, not assumed
Explainers lost to vibes. Short, motivational copy beat long, wordy copy by 8%.
04Impact

On-brand nature imagery outperformed image-less heroes by 20%. Lender trust badges below the hero drove a 12% uplift in homepage sign-ups. And the short-copy call I’d argued for won by 8% — instinct, then validated.

05Learnings

The result I’m proudest of wasn’t a number — it was learning when to trust a design instinct and how to make it safe: voice the conviction, then let the test settle it.

On-brand emotion converts, trust signals belong close to the ask, and when the data says mobile, everything is mobile-first. Seniority, here, was the confidence to disagree and the humility to prove it.
